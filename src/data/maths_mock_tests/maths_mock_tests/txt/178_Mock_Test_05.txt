Q1. Two finite sets have m and n elements respectively. The total number of subsets of first set is 56 more than the total number of subsets of the second set. The values of m and n respectively are.
    (A) 7, 6
    (B) 5, 1
    (C) 6, 3   <-- CORRECT
    (D) 8, 7
    Answer: C
    Explanation: Since, let A and B be such sets, i.e., n (A) = m, and n(B) = n
Thus, n (P(A)) = 2^m , n (P(B)) = 2^n
Therefore, n (P(A)) – n (P(B)) = 56, i.e., 2^m – 2^n = 56
$\Rightarrow$ 2^n (2^m–n – 1) = 2^3 7
$\Rightarrow$ n = 3, 2^m–n – 1 = 7
$\Rightarrow$ m = 6

Q20. $\lim \limits_{n \rightarrow \infty} \frac{1^{2}+2^{2}+3^{2}+\ldots+n^{2}}{n^{3}}$ is equal to:
    (A) $\frac {1}{2}$
    (B) $\frac {1}{3}$   <-- CORRECT
    (C) $\frac {1}{4}$
    (D) 0
    Answer: B
    Explanation: $\lim \limits_{n \rightarrow \infty} \frac{1^{2}+2^{2}+3^{2} \cdots \cdots n^{2}}{n^{3}}$
= $\lim \limits_{n \rightarrow \infty} \frac{\Sigma n^{2}}{n^{3}}$
= $\lim \limits_{n \rightarrow \infty} \frac{n(n+1)(2 n+1)}{6 n^{3}}$
= $\lim \limits_{n \rightarrow 0} \frac{(n+1)(2 n+1)}{6 n^{2}}$
Dividing the numerator and the denominator by n^2, we get
$\lim \limits_{n \rightarrow \infty} \frac{\frac{(n+1)}{n} \times \frac{(2 n+1)}{n}}{6}$
= $\lim \limits_{n \rightarrow \infty} \frac{\left(1+\frac{1}{n}\right)\left(2+\frac{1}{n}\right)}{6}$
= $\frac{2}{6}=\frac{1}{3}$
