Q1. The number of different ways in which a man can invite one or more of his 6 friends to dinner is?
    (A) 30
    (B) 15
    (C) 63   <-- CORRECT
    (D) 120
    Answer: C
    Explanation: He can invite anyone friend in ^6C_1 ways = 6 ways:
He can invite any two friends in ^6C_2 ways = 15 ways
He can invite any three friends in ^6C_3 ways = 20 ways
He can invite any 4 friends in ^6C_4ways = 15 ways
He can invite any 5 friends in ^6C_5 ways = 6 ways
He can invite all the 6 friends in ^6C_6 ways = 1 way.
Since any one of these could happen total possibilities are, 6 + 15 + 20 + 15 + 6 + 1 = 63.

    (A) 132
    (B) 164
    (C) 140   <-- CORRECT
    (D) 112
    Answer: C
    Explanation: Let the friends be A, B, C, D, E, F, G, H, I, J and assume A and B will not attend together.
Case 1: Both of them will not attend the party: Now we have to select the 6 guests from the remaining 8 members. 
Then no of ways is ^8C_6 = 28 ways.
Case 2: Either of them is selected for the party: Now we have to select the 5 guests from the remaining 8 members and one from A and B.
Then the no of ways = ^2C_1 $\times$ ^8C_5 = 112 ways.
Therefore total number of ways is 28 + 112 = 140 ways.

    (A) 90
    (B) 48
    (C) 120
    (D) 180   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: A team of 4 players are to be selected.
2 out of 6 men can be done in ^6C_2 ways. 2 out of 4 women can be done in ^4C_2 ways. 
So the number of ways to select 4 players is ^6C_2 $\times$ ^4C_2 = 90.
Now we can arrange these people to form mixed doubles.
If M_1,_ M_2,_ W_1,_ W_2, are the 4 members selected then one team can be chosen as (M_1, W_1) or (M_1, W_2) in 2 different ways.
Therefore the required number of arrangements = 90 $\times$ 2 = 180.
