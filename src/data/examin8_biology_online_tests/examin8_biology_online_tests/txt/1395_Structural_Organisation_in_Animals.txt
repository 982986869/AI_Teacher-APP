Q1. Where is ciliated epithelium found in the human body?
    (A) Fallopian tubes   <-- CORRECT
    (B) Oesophagus
    (C) Stomach
    (D) Intestines
    Answer: A
    Explanation: The ciliated epithelium is mainly present in the inner surface of hollow organs like bronchioles and fallopian tubes.

Q2. The epithelium found in the lining of the stomach and intestine is ________.
    (A) Squamous epithelium
    (B) Columnar epithelium   <-- CORRECT
    (C) Ciliated columnar epithelium
    (D) Cuboidal epithelium
    Answer: B
    Explanation: The columnar epithelium is found in the lining of the stomach and intestine and help in secretion and absorption. It also provides a barrier against harmful microorganisms.

Q3. The epithelium involved in diffusion is:
    (A) Cuboidal epithelium
    (B) Columnar epithelium
    (C) Squamous epithelium   <-- CORRECT
    (D) Ciliated columnar epithelium
    Answer: C
    Explanation: The squamous epithelium is made of a single thin layer of flattened cells with irregular boundaries. They are found in the walls of blood vessels and air sacs of the lungs and are involved in functions like forming a diffusion boundary.

Q4. Collagen is:
    (A) Lipid
    (B) Carbohydrate
    (C) Fibres of structural proteins   <-- CORRECT
    (D) Globular protein
    Answer: C
    Explanation: In all connective tissues except blood, the cells secrete fibres of structural proteins called collagen or elastin.

Q5. Fibres and fibroblasts are compactly packed in:
    (A) Areolar tissues
    (B) Dense irregular connective tissues   <-- CORRECT
    (C) Dense regular connective tissues
    (D) Loose connective tissues
    Answer: B
    Explanation: Dense irregular connective tissue provides strength to body parts. Hence the cells and fibres are packed compactly.

Q6. Tendons connect:
    (A) Ligaments to muscles
    (B) Bones to bones
    (C) Ligaments to bones
    (D) Muscles to bones   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: Tendons are dense connective tissue which provides strength and attach muscles to bones.

Q7. Which one of the following is not a connective tissue?
    (A) Bone
    (B) Nerve.   <-- CORRECT
    (C) Blood
    (D) Lymph
    Answer: B
    Explanation: Blood, bone, lymph forms the connective tissue that connects the different parts of the body
Nerve cells are parts of neural system or nervous system that transmit information from one part of the body to others.

Q8. The epithelium found in proximal convoluted tubule (PCT) of the nephron in the kidney is_______.
    (A) Cuboidal epithelium   <-- CORRECT
    (B) Columnar epithelium
    (C) Ciliated epithelium
    (D) Squamous epithelium
    Answer: A
    Explanation: The cuboidal epithelium is composed of a single layer of cube-like cells found in ducts of glands and tubular parts of nephrons in kidneys and its main functions are secretion and absorption.

Q9. What makes up more than one-half the volume of neural tissue in our body?
    (A) Plasma membrane
    (B) Neuroglia   <-- CORRECT
    (C) Neuron
    (D) Adjacent neurons
    Answer: B
    Explanation: The neuroglial cells which constitute the rest of the neural system protect and support neurons. Neuroglia makes up more than one-half the volume of neural tissue in our body.

Q10. Intercalated discs occur in:
    (A) In striped muscles
    (B) At the junction of muscle and nerve cells
    (C) Between cardiac muscle fibres   <-- CORRECT
    (D) Between neurons
    Answer: C
    Explanation: In between cardiac muscle fibres, there are communication junctions which are made up of intercalated discs.

Q11. Fibroblasts, macrophages, and mast cells are present in ______.
    (A) Areolar tissue   <-- CORRECT
    (B) Adipose tissue
    (C) Cartilage tissue
    (D) Glandular tissue
    Answer: A
    Explanation: Apart from being connective tissue, Areolar tissue is also protective in nature. It has a large number of fibroblast cells that produce fibres. Mast cells, plasma cells, and macrophages help to protect against pathogens and aid in tissue repair.

Q12. The fibre that can't be made to contract merely by thinking about it, is the:
    (A) Cardiac muscle tissue
    (B) Neural tissue
    (C) Smooth muscle fibres   <-- CORRECT
    (D) Skeletal muscle fibres
    Answer: C
    Explanation: Smooth muscles are ‘involuntary’ muscles as they can't be directly controlled. We usually are not able to make it contract merely by thinking about it as we can do with skeletal muscles. Involuntary muscles are under the control of medulla oblongata and part of the cerebrum. Smooth muscles are spindle-shaped or fusiform, uninucleated, and unstriated fibres. The wall of internal organs such as the blood vessels, stomach, and intestine contains this type of muscle tissue.

Q13. Identify the figure given below:
    (A) Salivary gland.   <-- CORRECT
    (B) Compound epithelium
    (C) Goblet cells of alimentary canal
    (D) Ciliated epithelium
    Answer: A
    Explanation: The figure is identified as salivary gland. It is multicellular and is lined with glandular epithelium cells. These types of cells are specialized in production and release of particular kind of digestive juices or sweat or oil. Salivary gland secretes alkaline fluid called saliva.

Q14. To which one of the following categories does adipose tissue belong?
    (A) Muscular
    (B) Connective   <-- CORRECT
    (C) Neural
    (D) Epithelial
    Answer: B
    Explanation: Adipose tissue is a type of connective tissue consisting mainly of adipose cells or adipocytes, specialized to synthesize and contain large globules of fat.

Q15. The junctions which help to stop substances from leaking across a tissue is:
    (A) Tight junctions   <-- CORRECT
    (B) Gap junctions
    (C) Adhering junctions
    (D) Specialized junctions
    Answer: A
    Explanation: Tight junctions hold cells together. They are narrow belts that circle around the upper part of lateral surfaces of the adjacent epithelial cells to create a fusion point. They stop substances from leaking across a tissue as they prevent the passage of molecules and ions through space between adjacent cells.

Q1. The most abundant type of animal tissue in the complex animals is:
    (A) Epithelial tissue
    (B) Nervous tissue
    (C) Connective tissue   <-- CORRECT
    (D) Muscle tissue
    Answer: C
    Explanation: The most abundant type of animal tissue connective tissue as it forms a variety of tissues such as blood, bone, cartilage, adipose, lymph, tendon, and ligament. These connective tissue types perform a wide range of functions in the animal body such as giving support, packing, repairing, storing of fat, connecting different organs, etc.

Q2. The formation of erythrocytes in foetus takes place in:
    (A) Liver and Spleen   <-- CORRECT
    (B) Sarcoplasm
    (C) Blood plasma
    (D) Red bone marrow
    Answer: A
    Explanation: In the early fetus, erythropoiesis takes place in the mesodermal cells of the yolk sac. Later, the production of RBCs starts in the fetal liver and increases until the 2nd^ trimester. After this, the rate of synthesis begins to decline in the liver as erythropoiesis increases in the bone marrow. RBCs are still produced in the liver for approximately 1 week after birth.

Q3. Study of tissues is called:
    (A) Anatomy
    (B) Metrology
    (C) Histology   <-- CORRECT
    (D) Cytology
    Answer: C
    Explanation: Study of organisms at tissues level is called histology. It includes the study of tissue structure, origin, location and function.

Q4. The epithelium covering in the tongue is:
    (A) Simple cuboidal
    (B) Pseudostratified
    (C) Squamous non-keratinized   <-- CORRECT
    (D) Squamous keratinized
    Answer: C
    Explanation: The tongue is covered by non-keratinized squamous epithelial tissues having taste buds of different kinds in different regions of the tongue.

Q5. Two animal cells are interconnected by:
    (A) Cell wall
    (B) Desmosomes   <-- CORRECT
    (C) Plasma membrane
    (D) Plamodesmata
    Answer: B
    Explanation: Two animal cells are interconnected by desmosomes, through which the exchange of materials takes place between two cells. It is formed from protein plaques in the cell membranes linked by filaments.

Q6. Myelin sheath is derived from:
    (A) Nerve cells
    (B) Schwann cells   <-- CORRECT
    (C) Neuroglial cells
    (D) Myocytes
    Answer: B
    Explanation: The outer layer of the axon of nerve cells is called the myelin sheath. Myelin sheath is derived from Schwann cells.

Q7. Antibodies are formed by:
    (A) Histiocytes
    (B) WBC
    (C) Mast cells
    (D) Plasma cells   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: Antibodies are formed against any foreign body entering the body. It is formed by plasma cells, globulins that develop resistance against a particular type of disease.

Q8. The cell lining the blood vessels are:
    (A) Smooth muscle tissue
    (B) Squamous epithelium   <-- CORRECT
    (C) Connective tissue
    (D) Columnar epithelium
    Answer: B
    Explanation: The endothelium is a type of epithelium that lines the interior surface of blood vessels and lymphatic vessels, forming an interface between circulating blood or lymph in the lumen and the rest of the vessel wall. It is a thin layer of simple squamous called endothelial cells.

Q9. Cardiac muscles are:
    (A) Smooth and voluntary
    (B) Striated and involuntary   <-- CORRECT
    (C) Smooth and involuntary
    (D) Unstriated and involuntary
    Answer: B
    Explanation: Cardiac muscle tissue is a contractile tissue present only in the heart. The cardiac muscle fibres are elongated, branched and uninucleate. They appear striated due to the presence of alternate light and dark band. Cardiac muscles are self-contracting and involuntary in nature. The contract in a rhythmic fashion and help the heart to pump the blood.

Q10. Which of the following structure is Ectodermal in origin?
    (A) Brain   <-- CORRECT
    (B) Notochord
    (C) Lungs
    (D) Kidney
    Answer: A
    Explanation: The ectoderm differentiates to form the nervous system (spine, peripheral nerves and brain), tooth enamel and the epidermis, the outer part of the integument. It also forms the lining of mouth, anus, nostrils, sweat glands, hair and nails.

Q11. A normal adult man and woman have ________ WBC per cubic millimetre.
    (A) 4.5 - 5 million   <-- CORRECT
    (B) 5.5 - 6 million
    (C) 1.5 – 3.5 million
    (D) 2.5 - 3 million
    Answer: A
    Explanation: A normal adult male and female have about 4.5 to 5 million of WBC per cubic millimetre of blood. The number of white blood cells increases during infection against pathogens.

Q12. The most active phagocytic white blood cells are:
    (A) Lymphocytes and macrophages
    (B) Eosinophils and neutrophils
    (C) Neutrophils and monocytes   <-- CORRECT
    (D) Neutrophils and eosinophils
    Answer: C
    Explanation: The most active phagocytic white blood cells are neutrophils and monocytes. These cells try to engulf the microbes that enter the blood to cause disease.

Q13. Nasal septum contains:
    (A) Elastic cartilage
    (B) Calcified cartilage
    (C) Fibrous cartilage
    (D) Hyaline cartilage   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: The nasal septum of human beings consists of hyaline cartilage which is highly elastic having collagen. It contains no nerves or blood vessels and is pearly bluish in colour.

Q14. Lymph contains:
    (A) Serum and WBC   <-- CORRECT
    (B) WBC and RBC
    (C) Platelet and serum
    (D) Blood plasma
    Answer: A
    Explanation: Lymph contains serum and WBC. Lymph is called extracellular fluid that helps in the transport of fat and protein. It contains WBC, so also help in protection against the infection.

Q15. Intercalated discs occur:
    (A) In striped muscles
    (B) At the junction of muscle and nerve cells
    (C) Between neurons
    (D) Between cardiac muscle fibres   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: Intercalated discs are microscopic identifying features of cardiac muscles fibres. Individual heart muscle cells are connected by intercalated discs to work as a single functional organ.

Q1. Basophils help the body defence by:
    (A) Phagocytosis of pathogens
    (B) Inhibit allergic
    (C) Cell mediated and antibody immunity
    (D) Heparin formation for preventing thrombosis   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: Basophils appear in many specific kinds of inflammatory reactions, particularly those that cause allergic symptoms. Basophils contain anticoagulant heparin, which prevents blood from clotting too quickly. (Thrombosisis the formation of a blood clot inside a blood vessel, obstructing the flow of blood through the circulatory system.) They also contain the vasodilator histamine, which promotes blood flow to tissues.
