Q1. The position of an object moving in a straight line can be specified with reference to
    (A) a triangle
    (B) an arbitrary star
    (C) another straight line
    (D) a conveniently chosen origin   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: To describe motion along a straight line, we can choose an axis, say X-axis, so that it coincides with the path of the object. We then measure the position of the object with reference to a conveniently chosen origin, say O. Positions to the right of O are taken as positive and to the left of O, as negative.

Q2. For motion in a straight line
    (A) motion towards origin is positive
    (B) position to the left of the origin is taken as positive and to the right as negative.
    (C) motion away from origin is positive
    (D) position to the right of the origin is taken as positive and to the left as negative.   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: To describe motion along a straight line, we can choose any axis, let us consider X-axis, so that it coincides with the path of the object. We then measure the position of the object with reference to a origin, say O. Positions to the right of O are taken as positive and to the left of O, as negative.

Q3. Path length is defined as
    (A) distance from origin to origin.
    (B) the distance from origin to maximum point.
    (C) the total length of the path traversed by an object.   <-- CORRECT
    (D) the distance from origin to minimum point.
    Answer: C
    Explanation: The path length is defined as the total length of the path traversed by an object. Unlike displacement, which is the shortest distance between two points, the path length is the total distance travelled, regardless of where it travelled.

Q4. Path length is a________________.
    (A) tensor
    (B) derived unit
    (C) scalar   <-- CORRECT
    (D) vector
    Answer: C
    Explanation: Path length has no particular direction and it depends upon the path chosen to reach the destination, so it is scaler.

Q5. In Kinematics we study ways to
    (A) describe motion without going into the causes of motion.   <-- CORRECT
    (B) find jerk without going into the causes of motion.
    (C) find acceleration without going into the causes of motion.
    (D) find velocity without going into the causes of motion.
    Answer: A
    Explanation: Kinematics is a branch of classical mechanics that describes the motion of points, bodies (objects), and systems of bodies (groups of objects) without considering the mass of each or the forces that caused the motion.

Q6. For motion in 3 dimensions we need
    (A) frame of reference consisting of a clock and a Cartesian system having two mutually $\perp$ axes (X and Y)
    (B) frame of reference consisting of a clock and a Cartesian system having three mutually $\perp$ axes, (X,Y, and Z)   <-- CORRECT
    (C) No frame of reference is required a single point will do.
    (D) No frame of reference is required ; a set of 3 points will do.
    Answer: B
    Explanation: Motion is a change in position of an object with time. In order to specify the position, we need to use a reference point and a set of axes. It is convenient to choose a rectangular coordinate system consisting of three mutually perpendicular axes, labelled X-, Y-, and Z- axes.
The point of intersection of these three axes is called origin (O) and serves as the reference point. The coordinates (x, y. z) of an object describe the position of the object with respect to this coordinate system.
To measure time, we position a clock in this system. This coordinate system along with a clock constitutes a frame of reference.

Q9. An object is said to be in uniform motion in a straight line if its displacement
    (A) is equal in equal intervals of time.   <-- CORRECT
    (B) is increasing in equal intervals of time.
    (C) is equal in not equal intervals of time.
    (D) is decreasing in equal intervals of time.
    Answer: A
    Explanation: Uniform motion is the kind of motion in which a body covers equal displacement in equal intervals of time. It does
not matter how small the time intervals are, as long as the displacements covered are equal. If a body is involved in rectilinear motion and the motion is uniform, then the acceleration of the body must be zero.

Q10. The change in position or displacement ($\Delta {\rm{x}}$) divided by the time intervals ($\Delta {\rm{t}}$), in which the displacement occurs is known as
    (A) average acceleration
    (B) acceleration
    (C) average velocity   <-- CORRECT
    (D) speed

$V_{average}= {∆x\over ∆t}$
Displacement is the straight line distance between the starting point and ending point of an object's motion.
Velocity is referred to as a vector quantity because it has both magnitude and direction.

Q13. Instantaneous velocity or simply velocity v at an instant t equals
    (A) $\mathop {\lim }\limits_{{\rm{t}} \to 0} \frac{{\Delta x}}{{2\Delta t}}$
    (B) $\mathop {\lim }\limits_{{\rm{t}} \to 1} \frac{{\Delta x}}{{\Delta t}}$
    (C) $\mathop {\lim }\limits_{{\rm{t}} \to \infty } \frac{{\Delta x}}{ {\Delta t}}$
    (D) $\mathop {\lim }\limits_{{\rm{t}} \to 0} \frac{{\Delta x}}{{\Delta t}}$   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: Instantaneous velocity is the velocity of an object in motion at a specific point in time. This is determined similarly to average velocity, but we narrow the period of time so that it approaches zero. The formula for instantaneous velocity is the limit as t approaches zero of the change in position over the change in t. Mathematically,
$\mathop {\lim }\limits_{{\rm{t}} \to 0} \frac{{\Delta x}}{{\Delta t}}$
The Instantaneous Velocity is expressed in m/s.

In terms of a displacement-time (x vs. t) graph, the instantaneous velocity (or, simply, velocity) can be thought of as the slope of the tangent line to the curve at any point.

Q3. Instantaneous acceleration is defined as
    (A) $\frac{v}{t}$
    (B) $\frac{{d^2}v} {{d{t^2}}}$
    (C) $\mathop {\lim }\limits_{{\rm{t}} \to 0} \frac{\Delta v}{{\Delta t}}$   <-- CORRECT
    (D) $\frac{dx}{{dt}}$
    Answer: C
    Explanation: Instantaneous acceleration is measured over a "short" time interval. The word short in this context means infinitely small or infinitesimal — having no duration or extent whatsoever. It's a mathematical ideal that can can only be realized as a limit. The limit of a rate as the denominator approaches zero is called a derivative.
Instantaneous acceleration is then the limit of average acceleration as the time interval approaches zero — or alternatively, acceleration is the derivative of velocity.
$a=\lim _\limits{\mathrm{t} \rightarrow 0} \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}=\frac{d v}{d t}$

Q14. A ball starts from rest and accelerates at 0.500 m/s^2 while moving down an inclined plane 9.00 m long. When it reaches the bottom, the ball rolls up another plane, where, after moving 15.0 m, it comes to rest. What is the acceleration in m/s^2 along the second plane?
    (A) -0.3   <-- CORRECT
    (B) -0.330
    (C) -0.321
    (D) -0.345

    Explanation: For First Plane
Initial velocity u = 0
Acceleration a = 0.500 m/s^​​​​​​2
Distance covered s = 9.0 m
Final velocity v =?
We know
v^2 − u^2 = 2as
$\Rightarrow$ v^2−0 = 2 $\times$ 0.500 $\times$ 9.0
$\Rightarrow$ v^2 = 9

This is initial velocity for second plane

Q15. A ball starts from rest and accelerates at 0.500 m/s^2 while moving down an inclined plane 9.00 m long. When it reaches the bottom, the ball rolls up another plane, where, after moving 15.0 m, it comes to rest. What is the ball’s speed in m/s 8.00 m along the second plane?

    (B) 2.05 m/s   <-- CORRECT
    (C) 2.56 m/s
    (D) 2.23 m/s

    Explanation: For First Plane
Initial velocity u = 0
Acceleration a = 0.500 m/s^​​​​​​2
Distance covered s = 9.0 m
Final velocity v =?

v^2 - u^2 = 2as
$\Rightarrow$ v^2 - 0 = 2 $\times$ 0.500 $\times$ 9.0

$\Rightarrow$ v = 3 m/s
This is initial velocity for second plane
For second plane 
Initial velocity u = 3 m/s

Distance covered s = 15 m
Acceleration a = ?

$\Rightarrow$ a = - 9/30 = - 0.3 m/s^2

$\Rightarrow$ v^2 - 9 = 2 $\times$ (- 0.3) $\times$ 8.0
