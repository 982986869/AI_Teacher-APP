Q1. A rigid body is a body which:
    (A) deforms on the application of force.
    (B) becomes longer or shorter if force is applied.
    (C) does not deform on the application of force.   <-- CORRECT
    (D) does not maintain the same distance between any two points on it if force is applied.
    Answer: C
    Explanation: A rigid body is an idealization of a solid body in which deformation or change in shape is neglected on the application of deforming force.

Q2. In the pure translational motion of a rigid body:
    (A) at different instants of time, every particle of the body has the same velocity.
    (B) at any instant of time different particles of the body have different velocities.
    (C) at any instant of time velocity is dependent on the position vector of a point on the body.
    (D) at any instant of time, every particle of the body has the same velocity.   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: In translational motion when the body moves along a straight line or more exactly when every point of the body travels on parallel lines, thus at any instant of time every particle of the body has the same velocity.

Q3. In rotation of a rigid body about a fixed axis is that in which:
    (A) every particle of the body moves in a circle, which lies in a plane perpendicular to the axis and has its centre on the axis.   <-- CORRECT
    (B) every particle of the body moves in an ellipse, which lies in a plane perpendicular to the axis and has its focii on the axis.
    (C) every particle of the body moves at the same speed.
    (D) particles close to the axis have larger velocities.
    Answer: A
    Explanation: When a rigid body rotates about a fixed axis, all particles of the body except those which lies on the axis of rotation, move along circular paths in a plane perpendicular to the axis.

Q4. In precession such as that of a top:
    (A) the axis of rotation is fixed
    (B) the axis of rotation moves   <-- CORRECT
    (C) the axis of rotation oscillates vertically
    (D) the axis of rotation oscillates horizontally
    Answer: B
    Explanation: Precession is a change in the orientation of the rotational axis of a rotating body, so the orientation of the axis of rotation of Top change.

Q5. In general motion of a rigid body:
    (A) particles on the body always move around an axis in circles
    (B) both translation and rotation can be present   <-- CORRECT
    (C) only rotation is present
    (D) only translation is present

Q6. Let r_i be the position vector of the i^th particle having mass m_i and R be the position vector of the centre of mass. The formula for R is:
    (A) $\mathbf{R}=\frac{\sum^{m_1} \mathbf{r}_{\mathbf{i}}}{\sum^{m_3}}$
    (B) $\mathbf{R}=\frac{\sum^{m_1} \mathbf{r}_{\mathbf{i}}}{\sum^{m_1}}$

    Explanation: Let us consider a system consisting of N – particles of masses m_1, m_2,….. m_N having position vectors ${\vec r_1},{\vec r_2}$,...... $ {\vec r_N} $ respectively.
The total mass M of the system is given by 
M = m­_1 +m_2 +…….+ m_N 
We can generalize the definition of the position of centre of mass consisting of N particles, hence the position vector of centre of mass is given below:-
$\vec{R}=\frac{m_1 \vec{r}_1+m_2 \vec{r}_2+\ldots \ldots \ldots+m_N \vec{r}_N}{m_1+m_2+\ldots \ldots+m_N}=\frac{\sum_\limits{i=1}^N m_i \vec{r}_i}{\sum_\limits{i=1}^N m_i}=\frac{\sum_\limits{i=1}^N m_i \vec{r}_i}{M}$

Q7. the centre of mass of a system of particles moves
    (A) as if all the mass of the system was concentrated at the axis of rotation and all the external forces were absent
    (B) as if all the mass of the system was concentrated at the centre of mass and all the external forces were zero
    (C) as if all the mass of the system was concentrated at the centre of mass and all the external forces were applied at that point   <-- CORRECT
    (D) as if all the mass of the system was concentrated in a sphere around the centre of mass and all the external forces were zero
    Answer: C
    Explanation: The centre of mass of a system of particles moves as if all the mass of the system is concentrated at the centre and all the external forces were applied at that point. In order to ease the calculations involved in the dynamics of an extended body, we treat any extended body as the system of particles, and the motion of centre of mass of the system is calculated by taking the mass of the whole system to be concentrated at the centre of mass and the external forces acting on system are said to be acting on the centre of mass.

Q8. The total momentum of a system of particles is equal to:
    (A) the product of half the total mass of the system and the velocity of its centre of mass
    (B) the product of the total mass of the system and the speed of its centre of mass
    (C) the product of the total mass of the system and the velocity of its centre of mass   <-- CORRECT
    (D) the product of the total mass of the system and the average velocity of its centre of mass
    Answer: C
    Explanation: Let us consider a system of n particles of masses m_1, m_2, ….m_N. If M is the total mass of the system.
M = m_1 + m_2 +……..+ m_N
If ${\vec R}$ is the position vector of the centre of mass and ${\vec r_1},{\vec r_2},{\vec r_3} . ..... {\vec r_n}$those of constituent particles then
$\mathop R\limits^ \to = {{{m_1}{{\mathop r\limits^ \to }_1} + {m_2}{{\mathop r\limits^ \to }_2} + .... + {m_N}{{\mathop r\limits^ \to }_N}} \over {{m_1} + {m_2} + ..... + {m_N}}} $ = $ {{{m_1}{{\mathop r\limits^ \to }_1} + {m_2}{{\mathop r\limits^ \to }_2} + {m_N}{{\mathop r\limits^ \to }_N}} \over M}$ 
Differentiating both sides w.r.t. time t, we get
${{d\mathop R\limits^ \to } \over {dt}} $ = $ {1 \over M}\left[ {{m_1}{{d{{\mathop r\limits^ \to }_1}} \over {dt}} + {m_2}{{d{{\mathop r\limits^ \to }_2}} \over {dt}} + .......... + {m_N}{{d{{\mathop r\limits^ \to }_N}} \over {dt}}} \right]$ 
Let the velocity of centre of mass is ${{d\mathop R\limits^ \to } \over {dt}} = \mathop {{V_{CM}}}\limits^ \to $
${{d\mathop {{r_1}}\limits^ \to } \over {dt}} = {\mathop v\limits^ \to _1},{{d\mathop {{r_2}}\limits^ \to } \over {dt}} = {\mathop v\limits^ \to _{2,}}.....{{d\mathop {{r_n}}\limits^ \to } \over {dt}} = \mathop {{v_n}}\limits^ \to $ 
$M\mathop {{V_{CM}}}\limits^ \to $ = $ {m_1}{\mathop v\limits^ \to _1} + {m_2}{\mathop v\limits^ \to _2} + .... + {m_N}{\mathop v\limits^ \to _N} = \sum\limits_{i = 1}^N {{m_i}\,\,\,{{\mathop v\limits^ \to }_i}} $ 
Hence the total momentum of a system of particles is equal to the product of the total mass of the system and the velocity of its centre of mass.

Q1. Torque $\tau $ is defined in terms of the position vector $r$ and the force acting F as:
    (A) $\tau = r \times F.r$
    (B) $\tau = F + r$
    (C) $\tau = r.r \times F$
    (D) $\tau = r \times F$   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: Torque = Force $\times$ Perpendicular distance of the line of action of the force from the axis of rotation. It is also called the moment of force.
Torque is defined as the cross product of the vector by which the force's application point is offset relative to the fixed suspension point (distance vector) and the force vector, which tends to produce rotational motion.
$\vec \tau = \vec r \times \vec F$

Q2. The angular momentum L of the particle having a position vector $r$ and linear momentum $p$, with respect to the origin O is defined to be
    (A) $\vec L = \vec r \times \vec p.\vec \omega $
    (B) $L = \vec r \times \vec p \times \vec r$
    (C) $\vec L = \vec r \times \vec r \times \vec p$
    (D) $\vec L =\vec r \times\vec p$   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: Angular momentum is also referred to as a moment of linear momentum
The angular momentum L of a particle of mass m with respect to a chosen origin is given by
$\vec L = \vec r \times \vec p$ 
The direction of angular momentum is according to the right-hand rule.

Q3. Torque $\tau $ and angular momentum L are related by:
    (A) $\tau=\frac{d L}{d t}$   <-- CORRECT
    (B) $\tau=\frac{d^2 L}{d t^2}$
    (C) $\tau=2 \frac{d L}{d t}$
    (D) $\tau=\frac{d^3 L}{d t^3}$
    Answer: A
    Explanation: $\vec L = \vec r \times \vec p$ 
on differentiation with respect to time 
$\frac{d \vec{L}}{d t}=\frac{d}{d t}(\vec{r} \times \vec{p})$ 
Applying the product rule for differentiation to the right-hand side,
$\frac{d}{d t}(\vec{r} \times \vec{p})=\left(\frac{d \vec{r}}{d t} \times \vec{p}\right)+\left(\vec{r} \times \frac{d \vec{p}}{d t}\right)$
$\frac{d \vec{r}}{d t}=\vec{v}, \vec{p}=m \vec{v}$
$\left(\frac{d \vec{r}}{d t} \times \vec{p}\right)=\vec{v} \times m \vec{v}=0$
$\frac{d \vec{p}}{d t}=\vec{F}$
$\left(\vec{r} \times \frac{d \vec{p}}{d t}\right)=\vec{r} \times \vec{F}=\vec{\tau}$
Hence $\vec{\tau}=\frac{d \vec{L}}{d t}$

    (B) $\frac{mr{\omega ^2}}{2}$
    (C) mr ${\omega ^2}$
    (D) mr^2 ${\omega ^2}$
    Answer: A
    Explanation: The kinetic energy of the body in rotational motion is $KE = \frac{1}{2}I{\omega ^2} = \frac{1}{ 2}m{r^2}{\omega ^2}$ as a moment of inertia of ring about its central axis is I = mr^2.

Q10. A thin circular ring of mass M and radius R is rotating about its central axis with angular velocity. Four-point objects each of mass m are attached gently to the opposite ends of two perpendicular diameters, the angular velocity of the ring is given by:
    (A) $\frac{M}{{M + 4m}} \cdot \omega $   <-- CORRECT
    (B) $\frac{M}{{M + m}} \cdot \omega $
    (C) $\frac{M-4 m}{M+4 m} \cdot \omega$
    (D) $\frac{{M + 4m}} {M} \cdot \omega $
    Answer: A
    Explanation: Let $\omega$ be the angular velocity of the Ring of Mass M, thus the moment of inertia about a given axis is I_1 = $MR^2$ and the four-point objects are gently placed at perpendicular diameters at the opposite end, so, the distance of each object from the axis of rotation is R, so total moment of inertia of the ring and four objects is I_2 = $MR^2 + 4mR^2$. 
According to the law of conservation of angular momentum I_1 $\omega$ = I_2$\omega$_2 , So on solving $\omega_2=\left(\frac{M R^2}{M R^2+4 m R^2}\right) \omega=\frac{M}{M+4 m} \cdot \omega$

Q11. What is the moment of inertia of a thin rod of length L and mass M about an axis passing through one end and perpendicular to its length?
    (A) $\frac{1}{12}$ ML^2
    (B) $\frac{1}{2}$ML^2
    (C) $\frac{1}{3}$ ML^2   <-- CORRECT
    (D) ML^2
    Answer: C
    Explanation: Using theorem of the parallel axis, the axis is shifted by $\frac{L}{2}$ distance from the centre of Mass where the Moment of Inertia is $\frac{M L^2}{12}$, thus the moment of inertia of a thin rod about an axis passing through one end and perpendicular to its length is $\frac{M L^2}{12}+\frac{M L^2}{4}=\frac{1}{3} M L^2$

Q12. The radius of gyration of a solid disc about one of its diameter is given by:
    (A) $\frac{R}{2}$   <-- CORRECT
    (B) 2R
    (C) $\sqrt {2} $R
    (D) $\frac{R}{{\sqrt 2 }}$
    Answer: A
    Explanation: Moment of inertia of an axis passing through its center and perpendicular to its plane:
I = $\frac{1}{2}$ MR^2
Using perpendicular axis theorem Ix +Iy = Iz, so 2I_d = $\frac{1}{2}$ MR^2
Moment of inertia of along its diameter I_d = $\frac{1}{4}$MR^2
And the radius of gyration MK^2 = $\frac{1}{4}$MR^2 
K = $\frac{R}{2}$

Q13. The moment of inertia of a solid sphere of density $\rho $ and radius R is given by:
    (A) $\frac{176} {{105}}\rho {R^2}$
    (B) $\frac{176}{{105}}\rho {R^5}$   <-- CORRECT
    (C) $\frac{176}{{105}}\rho {R^3}$
    (D) $\frac{105}{{176}}\rho {R^2}$
    Answer: B
    Explanation: $I=\frac{2}{5}\left(M R^{2}\right)$
$=\frac{2}{5}\left[\left(\frac{4}{3} \pi R^{3}\right) \cdot \rho . R^{2}\right]$
$=\frac{2}{5}\left[\left(\frac{4}{3} \frac{22}{7} R^{3}\right) \cdot \rho . R^{2}\right]$ 
As Mass = Density $\times $ Volume of Sphere 
$=\frac{176}{{105}}\rho {R^5}$

Q14. A thin uniform rod of length 2l and mass M is acted upon a constant torque. The angular velocity changes from zero to $\omega $ in time t. The value of torque is:
    (A) $\frac{M{l^2}\omega } {t}$
    (B) $\frac{M{l^2}\omega }{{12t}}$
    (C) $\frac{2M{l^2}\omega } {{3t}}$
    (D) $\frac{M{l^2}\omega }{{3t}}$   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: As Torque($\tau $) is equal to the product of Moment of Inertia (I) and Angular acceleration $\left( \alpha \right)$
$\tau=I \alpha$
 $\tau=I \frac{\Delta \omega}{\Delta t}$
$\tau=\left[\frac{M(2 l)^{2}}{12}\right]\left[\frac{\omega}{t}\right]$
$\tau=\frac{M l^{2} \omega}{3 t}$

Q15. A particle is orbiting in a vertical plane. Its linear momentum will be directed:
    (A) Horizontally
    (B) Vertically
    (C) Tangential to the orbit   <-- CORRECT
    (D) At 45$^\circ $ to the vertical
    Answer: C
    Explanation: As the direction of velocity at any point moving in a circular orbit is tangent at that point, thus momentum would be tangential to the orbit.
