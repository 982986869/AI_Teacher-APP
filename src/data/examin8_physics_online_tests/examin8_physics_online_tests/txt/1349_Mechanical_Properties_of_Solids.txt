Q1. A perfectly rigid body is one:
    (A) which starts flowing like water on the application of force.
    (B) which does not move on the application of force.
    (C) whose shape and size do not change on application of force.   <-- CORRECT
    (D) whose shape and size change on application of force.
    Answer: C
    Explanation: A perfectly rigid body is hypothetical in nature but for some phenomena ( in rotational bodies ) we assume bodies are perfectly rigid i.e. the intermolecular forces are always in equilibrium irrespective of the external forces due to which their shape and size are constant.

Q2. Elasticity is the property of a body, by virtue of which:
    (A) it changes size and shape when the force is applied and stays in that shape when the applied force is removed.
    (B) it tends to regain its original size and shape when the applied force is removed.   <-- CORRECT
    (C) it is distorted or stretches without the application of force.
    (D) it remains in original size and shape when the force is applied.
    Answer: B
    Explanation: When an external force is applied to the solid bodies, the solid bodies get deformed. The atoms or molecules are displaced from their equilibrium positions causing a change in the interatomic (or intermolecular) distances. When the deforming force is removed, the interatomic forces tend to drive them back to their original positions. Thus the body regains its original shape and size.

    (C) $\sqrt {2g{R_E}} $   <-- CORRECT
    (D) $\sqrt {5g{R_E}} $

$W = G{{Mm} \over {R_e{\rm{ }}}}$

$\therefore {1 \over 2}{\rm{ }}m{v_e}^2 = G{{Mm} \over R_e}$
$ \Rightarrow {v_e} = \sqrt {2g{R_e}} $

Q5. Bulk modulus is:
    (A) the ratio of longitudinal stress to the corresponding hydraulic strain.
    (B) the ratio of lateral stress to the corresponding hydraulic strain.
    (C) the ratio of hydraulic strain to the corresponding hydraulic stress.
    (D) the ratio of hydraulic stress to the corresponding hydraulic strain.   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: When a body is submerged in a fluid, it undergoes hydraulic stress (equal in magnitude to the hydraulic pressure). This leads to a decrease in the volume of the body thus producing a strain called volume strain. The ratio of hydraulic stress to the corresponding hydraulic strain is called bulk modulus. It is denoted by symbol B.
Hence, bulk modulus = hydraulic or normal stress/ volumetric strain
$B = {- p\over ({\delta V \over V})}$ 
The negative sign indicates the fact that with an increase in pressure, a decrease in volume occurs.

Q12. A steel wire of length 4.7 m and cross-sectional area 3.0 $ \times $ 10^-5 m^2 stretch by the same amount as a copper wire of length 3.5 m and cross-sectional area of 4.0 $ \times $ 10^−5 m^2 under a given load. What is the ratio of Young’s modulus of steel to that of copper?
    (A) 2.0
    (B) 1.2
    (C) 1.8   <-- CORRECT
    (D) 1.6
    Answer: C
    Explanation: Length of the steel wire, L_1 = 4.7 m
Area of cross-section of the steel wire, A1 = 3.0 $\times$ 10^-5 m^2
Length of the copper wire, L_2 = 3.5 m
Area of cross-section of the copper wire, A_2 = 4.0 $\times$ 10^-5 m^2
Change in length = $\Delta$L_1 = $\Delta$L_2 = $\Delta$L
Force applied in both the cases = F
Young’s modulus of the steel wire:
Y_1 = ($F_1 \over A_1$) ($L_1 \over \Delta L_1$)
= ($F \over 3 \times 10^{-5}$) ($4.7 \over \Delta L$) ….(i)
Young’s modulus of the copper wire:
Y_2 = ($F_2 \over A_2$) ($L_2 \over ΔL_2 $)
= ($F \over 4 \times 10^{-5}$) ($3.5 \over \Delta L$) ….(ii)
Dividing (i) by (ii), we get:
$Y_1 \over Y_2$ = $(4.7 \times 4 \times 10^{-5}) \over (3 \times 10^{-5} \times 3.5)$
= 1.79 : 1
The ratio of Young’s modulus of steel to that of copper is 1.8:1.

    (C) 2.5 $ \times $ 10^−4 m^3

    Answer: D
    Explanation: bulk modulus is given by $B=\frac{\Delta P}{\frac{\Delta V}{V}} \Rightarrow \Delta V=\frac{\Delta P V}{B}$ $\Rightarrow$ (1)

pressure at bottom will be p_2 = P_atm + P_water = P_atm + (1.1 $\times$ 10^8)
$\Delta P=p_{2}-p_{1}$ = 1.1 $\times$ 10^8pa
substitute values in equation 1
$\Delta V=\frac{1.1 \times 10^{8} \times 0.32}{160 \times 10^{9}}$

Q1. What diameter should a 10-m-long steel wire have if we do not want it to stretch more than 0.5 cm under a tension of 940 N? Take Young's modulus of steel as 20 $ \times $ 10^10 Pa.
    (A) 3.2 mm
    (B) 3.0 mm
    (C) 3.6 mm
    (D) 3.4 mm   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: Given length of wire L = 10 m
elongation in wire $\Delta L$ = 0.5 cm = 0.5 $\times$ 10^-2 m
restoring force in wire i.e. tension T = 940 N
young modulus of wire Y = 20 $\times$ 10^10N/m^2
using relation for young modulus $Y = \frac{s t r e s s}{s t r a i n} = \frac{T \times L}{A \times \Delta L}$
$A = \frac{T \times L}{Y \times \Delta L} = \frac{940 \times 10}{20 \times 10^{10} \times 0.5 \times 10^{-2}}$
$\pi \mathrm{R}^{2} = 940 \times 10^{-8}$
R = 17.30 $\times$ 10^-4
Diameter d = 2 R $\Rightarrow$ 2 $\times$ 17.30 $\times$ 10^-4
d = 34.60 $\times$ 10^-4 m $\approx$ 3.4 mm

By how much does this volume change once the sphere is submerged? ( take the modulus of brass as 61 GPa.)

    (C) -1.6 $ \times $ 10^-4 m^3   <-- CORRECT

Q11. You hang a flood lamp from the end of vertical steel wire. The flood lamp stretches the wire 0.18 mm and the stress is proportional to the strain. How much would it have stretched if the wire were twice as long?

    (C) 0.40 mm
    (D) 0.34 mm
    Answer: B
    Explanation: Let length in the first case is L
extension $\Delta L$ = 0.18 mm
length in 2 nd case will be 2L
given that stress $\alpha$ strain $\Rightarrow $ stress = k $\times$ strain (k is proportionality constant)
as stress in both cases is same because same flood lamp is hung and
the diameter of wire remains same on increasing length to 2L thus force per unit area
i.e. stress will be the same. so
$\frac{\text {stress}_{1}}{\text {stress}_{2}}=\frac{k \times \text {strain}_{1}}{k \times \text {strain}_{2}}$ $\Rightarrow \frac{\operatorname{strain}_{1}}{\operatorname{strain}_{2}}$ = 1 (stress_1 = stress_2)

Q14. A 12.0-kg mass, fastened to the end of aluminum wire with an unstretched length of 0.50 m, is whirled in a vertical circle with a constant angular speed of 120 rev/min. The cross-sectional area of the wire is 0.014 cm^2. Calculate the elongation of the wire when the mass is at the lowest point of the path (young modulus of steel = 0.70 $\times$ 10^11 N/m^2)?
    (A) 0.56 cm
    (B) 0.50 cm
    (C) 0.52 cm
    (D) 0.54 cm   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: Given mass m = 12.0 kg
length of aluminium wire L = 0.50 m
angular speed $\omega$ = 120 rev/min = 120 $\times$ $\frac{2 \pi}{60} = 4 \pi$ rad/s
area of wire A = 0.014 cm^2 = 0.014 $\times$ 10^-4m^2
when whirled in vertical circle at lowest point let tension in wire be T
applying net force = centripetal force
T - mg = $\mathrm{m} \omega^{2} \mathrm{r}$
T= (12 $\times$ (4$\pi$)^2 $\times$0.50) + 12 $\times$ 9.8
T = 1064.12N
young modulus y = $\frac{\mathrm{stress}}{\mathrm{strain}}=\frac{\frac{\mathrm{T}}{\mathrm{A}}}{\frac{\Delta \mathrm{L}}{\mathrm{L}}}$ $\Rightarrow$ $\Delta \mathrm{L}=\frac{\mathrm{T} \times \mathrm{L}}{\mathrm{A} \times \mathrm{y}}$
$\Delta \mathrm{L}=\frac{1064.12 \times 0.50}{0.014 \times 10^{-4} \times 0.70 \times 10^{11}}$
$\Delta \mathrm{L}$ = 54291.91 $\times$ 10^-7 m = 0.54 cm
