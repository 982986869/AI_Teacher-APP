Q1. All planets move in elliptical orbits with the Sun situated at one of the foci of the ellipse. This law was given by
    (A) Maxwell
    (B) Newton
    (C) Einstein
    (D) Kepler   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: In the 17th century, Johannes Kepler discovered that the orbits along which the planets travel around the Sun are ellipses with the Sun at one focus, and described this in his first law of planetary motion.

Q2. According to Kepler’s Law of areas:
    (A) Only the line that joins earth to the sun sweeps equal areas in equal intervals of time
    (B) The line that joins any planet to the sun sweeps equal areas in equal intervals of time   <-- CORRECT
    (C) Only the line that joins mars to the earth sweeps equal areas in equal intervals of time
    (D) The line that joins any planet to the earth sweeps equal areas in equal intervals of time
    Answer: B
    Explanation: Kepler law of areas describes the speed of a planet travelling in an elliptical orbit around the sun. A planet’s orbital speed changes, depending on how far it is from the Sun. The closer a planet is to the Sun, the stronger the Sun’s gravitational pull on it, and the faster the planet moves. The farther it is from the Sun, the weaker the Sun’s gravitational pull, and the slower it moves in its orbit. So, the statement of Kepler's law of area is "The line joining the sun to the planet sweeps out equal areas in equal interval of time". i.e. areal velocity is constant.

Q3. According to Kepler’s Law of periods, the ________ of the time period of revolution of a planet is proportional to the cube of the _________ of the ellipse traced out by the planet.
    (A) cube, semi-major axis
    (B) square, semi-minor axis
    (C) square, semi-major axis   <-- CORRECT
    (D) cube, semi-minor axis
    Answer: C
    Explanation: Kepler's 3^rd Law: T^2 = a^3. Kepler's 3rd law is a mathematical formula. It means that if you know the period of a planet's orbit (T = how long it takes the planet to go around the Sun), then you can determine that planet's distance from the Sun (a is the length of the semimajor axis of the planet's orbit)

Q4. The area $\Delta $A swept out by a planet of mass m in time interval $\Delta $t is related to the angular momentum by:
    (A) $\Delta {{A}}/\Delta {\rm{t}}$ = $L / (2 m)$   <-- CORRECT
    (B) $\Delta {\bf{A}}/\Delta {\rm{t}}$ = $GL / (2 m)$
    (C) $\Delta {{A}}/\Delta {\rm{t}}$ = $2L / m$
    (D) $\Delta {{A}}/\Delta {\rm{t}}$ =$ gL / (2 m)$
    Answer: A
    Explanation: The law of areas can be understood as the consequence of conservation of angular momentum which is valid for any central force such that the force on the planet is along the vector joining the sun and the planet. Let the sun be at the origin and let the position and momentum of the planet be denoted by r and p respectively. Then the area swept out by the planet of mass m in the time interval Δt is given by ΔA = $\frac{1}{2}$ (r $\times$ vΔt).
Hence $\frac{\Delta{A}}{\Delta{t}}$ = $\frac{1}{2}$(r $\times$ p)/m = $\frac{L}{2m}$
where v is the velocity, L is the angular momentum equal to (r $\times$ p).

Q7. A central force is always directed:
    (A) at a varying angle to the position vector of the point of application of the force with respect to the fixed point
    (B) along the position vector of the point of application of the force with respect to the fixed point   <-- CORRECT
    (C) at a fixed angle to the position vector of the point of application of the force with respect to the fixed point
    (D) perpendicular to the position vector of the point of application of the force with respect to the fixed point
    Answer: B
    Explanation: The motion of a particle under a central force F always remains in the plane defined by its initial position and velocity. This may be seen by symmetry. Since the position r, velocity v, and force F all lie in the same plane, there is never an acceleration perpendicular to that plane, because that would break the symmetry between "above" the plane and "below" the plane.
To demonstrate this mathematically, it suffices to show that the angular momentum of the particle is constant. This angular momentum L is defined by the equation
L = r $\times$ p = r $\times$ mv
as angular momentum is conserved, the torque vanishes as F is zero or along r
$dL\over dt$= $r \times F$ = 0
central forces always satisfy this condition. A central force is always directed towards the center.

Q10. The force of attraction between a hollow spherical shell of uniform density and a point mass situated outside is just as if the entire mass of the shell is
    (A) equally concentrated at four points on a square of the shell.
    (B) concentrated at the centre of the shell.   <-- CORRECT
    (C) equally concentrated at opposite ends of the diameter of the sphere.
    (D) equally concentrated at three points on a triangle of the shell.
    Answer: B
    Explanation: According to Shell's theorem, If a particle of mass m is located outside a spherical shell of mass M at, for instance, point P, the shell attracts the particle as though the mass of the shell were concentrated at its centre. Thus, as far as the gravitational force acting on a particle outside the shell is concerned, a spherical shell acts no differently from the solid spherical distributions of mass.

Q12. In Cavendish’s experiment:
    (A) net torque on bar AB having two small lead spheres due to gravitational forces is positive
    (B) torque on bar AB having two small lead spheres due to gravitational forces is balanced by the restoring torque of the wire   <-- CORRECT
    (C) the net force on bar AB having two small lead spheres due to gravitational forces is negative
    (D) the net force on bar AB having two small lead spheres due to gravitational forces is positive
    Answer: B
    Explanation: Cavendish's apparatus for experimentally determining the value of G involved a light, rigid rod about 2-feet long. Two small lead spheres were attached to the ends of the rod and the rod was suspended by a thin wire. When the rod becomes twisted, the torsion of the wire begins to exert a torsional force that is proportional to the angle of rotation of the rod. The more twist of the wire, the more the system pushes backward to restore itself towards the original position.

Q13. The value of the gravitational constant G is
    (A) G = 6.67$ \times $${\rm{1}}{0^{ - {\rm{11}}}}$N m$^{\rm{2}}$/kg$^{\rm{2}}$   <-- CORRECT
    (B) G = 6.67$ \times $${\rm{1}}{0^{ - {\rm{11}}}}$ N m/kg$^{\rm{2}}$
    (C) G = 6.67$ \times $${\rm{1}}{0^{ - {\rm{11}}}}$ m$^{\rm{2}}$/kg$^{\rm{2}}$
    (D) G = 6.67$ \times $${\rm{1}}{0^{ - {\rm{11}}}}$ N m$^{\rm{2}}$/kg
    Answer: A
    Explanation: The value of G was experimentally determined by Lord Henry Cavendish using a torsion balance. Cavendish brought two large lead spheres near the smaller spheres attached to the rod. Since all masses attract, the large spheres exerted a gravitational force upon the smaller spheres and twisted the rod a measurable amount. Once the torsional force balanced the gravitational force, the rod and spheres came to rest and Cavendish was able to determine the gravitational force of attraction between the masses. Cavendish expressed his result in terms of the density of the Earth.
$G = g{{R_{earth}^2} \over {{M_{earth}}}} = {{3g} \over {4\pi {R_{earth}}{\rho _{earth}}}}$
After converting to SI units, Cavendish's value for the Earth's density, 5.448 g cm^−3, gives
G = 6.74×10^−11 m^3 kg^–1 s^−2
Today, the currently accepted value is 6.67259 x 10^-11 N m^2/kg^2.

Q14. The acceleration due to gravity at a height h in terms of mass of earth M_E, radius of the earth R_E and gravitational constant G is
    (A) $g(h)=\frac{G M_E}{\left(R_E+2 h\right)^2}$
    (B) $g(h)=\frac{M_E}{\left(R_E+h\right)^2}$
    (C) $g(h)=\frac{G M_E}{\left(R_E+h\right)^2}$   <-- CORRECT
    (D) $g(h)=\frac{G m M_E}{\left(R_E+h\right)^2}$
    Answer: C
    Explanation: We know by Newton's law of gravitation, the force on the body of mass m, situated at height h to the surface of the earth of mass M_E is given by:
$F=G \frac{M_e m}{(r+h)^2}$ ...(i)
We also know F = mass $\times$ acceleration(here acceleration is the acceleration due to gravity)
$\Rightarrow$ F = mg ...(ii)
Equating (i) & (ii), we get
$g=G \frac{M_e}{(r+h)^2}$

Q15. The acceleration due to gravity at a depth d in terms of g(acceleration due to gravity) at radius of the earth R_E is
    (A) $g(d) = g(1 + \frac{d}{2R_E})$
    (B) $g(d) = g(1 + \frac{d}{R_E})$

    (D) $g(d) = g(1 - \frac{d}{ R_E})$   <-- CORRECT

$g = G\frac{M}{{{R_E^2}}} = \frac{4}{3}\pi \rho GR_E$ ...(1)

$g(d) = \frac{4}{ 3}\pi \rho G(R - d)$ ...(2)

Q1. The formula for gravitational potential energy associated with two particles of masses m_1 and m_2 separated by distance r is given by
    (A) ${\rm{v}} = - \frac{G{m_1}{m_2}}{r}$   <-- CORRECT
    (B) ${\rm{v}} = - \frac{G{m_1}}{r}$
    (C) $\mathrm{v}=-\frac{G m_1 m_2}{2 r}$
    (D) $\mathrm{v}=-\frac{G m_2}{r}$
    Answer: A
    Explanation: Change in gravitational potential energy of a system is defined as the -ve of the work done by the gravitational force as the configuration of the system is changed.
${U_f}\; - {\rm{ }}{U_i}\; = {\rm{ }}{W_{gr}}=V(Potential Energy)$ 
Change in gravitational potential energy of two point masses m_1 and m_2 as their separation is changed from r_1 to r_2 is given by
$U\left(r_2\right)-U\left(r_1\right)=G m_1 m_2\left[\frac{1}{r_1}-\frac{1}{r_2}\right]$ 
If, at infinite separation, gravitational potential energy is assumed to be zero, then the gravitational potential energy of the above two point mass system at separation r,
$U(r) = - G\frac{{m_1}{m_2}} {r}$ 
$\therefore V = - G\frac{{m_1}{m_2}} { r}$

Q6. The total energy of a circularly orbiting satellite is

    (B) negative   <-- CORRECT
    (C) positive large
    (D) positive small
    Answer: B

$ \begin{gathered} {\text{Total Energy = Potential Energy(U) + Kinetic Energy(K)}} \hfill \\ {\text{ = }}\frac{{ - GMm}}{r} + \frac{{GMm}}{{2r}} \hfill \\ {\text{ = }}\frac{{ - GMm}}{{2r}} \hfill \\ \end{gathered} $

Q14. A particle of mass 3m is located 1.00 m from a particle of mass m. Where should you put a third mass M so that the net gravitational force on M due to the two masses is exactly zero?
    (A) 0.534 m from the mass m
    (B) 0.234 m from the mass 3m
    (C) 0.63 m from the mass 3m   <-- CORRECT
    (D) 0.69 m from the mass m
    Answer: C
    Explanation: Let x be the distance of the third particle from the mass 3m. then (1 - x) is the distance will be the distance from mass m.
If we require that net force on another mass M be zero, then we must have the following:
$\frac{{GM(3m)}}{{{x^2}}} = \frac{{GM(m)}}{{{{\left( {1 - x} \right)}^2}}}$
$\Rightarrow \frac{3}{{{x^2}}} = \frac{1}{{{{\left( {1 - x} \right)}^2}}}$
$\Rightarrow {x^2} = 3 \times {\left( {1 - x} \right)^2}$
$\Rightarrow x = \sqrt {3 \times {{\left( {1 - x} \right)}^2}} = \sqrt 3 (1 - x) $
$\Rightarrow x = \frac{{\sqrt 3 }}{{1 + \sqrt 3 }} = 0.63$
$\therefore$ The particle is 0.63 m away from 3m.

Q1. Titania, the largest moon of the planet Uranus, has $\frac 1 8$ the radius of the earth and $\frac {1} {1700}$ the mass of the earth. What is the acceleration due to gravity at the surface of Titania? Data: G = 6.67$ \times $ 10^-11 N m$^{\rm{2}}$/kg$^{\rm{2}}$, R_E = 6.38 $ \times $ ${\rm{1}}{0^{\rm{6}}}$m, m_E = 5.97 $ \times $ 10^24 kg.
    (A) 0.43 m/s$^{\rm{2}}$ Hz
    (B) 0.28 m/s^2
    (C) 0.18 m/s^2
    (D) 0.37 m/s^2   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: $g_{t}=G \frac{M_{t}}{R_{t}^{2}}, g_{E}=G \frac{M_{E}}{R_{E}^{2}}=9.8 \mathrm{m} / \mathrm{s}^{2}$
$\Rightarrow g_{t}=G \frac{\left(\frac{M_{E}}{1700}\right)}{\left(\frac{R_{E}}{8}\right)^{2}} $
$=\frac{64}{1700} G \frac{M_{E}}{R_{E}^{2}}=\frac{64}{1700} \times g_{E}$
= 0.37m/s^2

Q4. How will you ‘weigh the sun’, that is estimate its mass? The mean orbital radius of the earth around the sun is 1.5 $ \times $ 10^8 km.

T = Time Period Of Earth Around The Sun = 365 Days 
= 365 $\times$ 24 $\times$ 60 $\times$ 60 sec
G = 6.67 $\times$ 10^11 Nm^2kg^-2 From the relation,
$\frac{R}{T^{2}}=\frac{G M_{s}}{4 \pi^{2} R^{2}}$ where M_s = Mass of sun = ?
$\Rightarrow \mathrm{M}_{s}=\frac{4 \pi^{2} \mathrm{R}^{3}}{\mathrm{T}^{2} G}$

$\therefore
$ Mass of sun = 2.0 $\times$ 10^30 kg

Q6. A body weighs 63 N on the surface of the earth. What is the gravitational force on it due to the earth at a height equal to half the radius of the earth?
    (A) 180 N
    (B) 128 N
    (C) 28 N   <-- CORRECT
    (D) 18 N
    Answer: C
    Explanation: ${g_h} = g{\left( {1 + \frac{h}{R}} \right)^{ - 2}}$
Given, h = $\frac{R}{2}$
$ \therefore {g_h} = g{\left( {1 + \frac{{\frac{{\text{R}}}{2}}}{R}} \right)^{ - 2}}$
$\Rightarrow \frac{{{g_h}}}{g} = \frac{1}{{{{\left( {1 + \frac{1}{2}} \right)}^2}}} = \frac{1}{{{{\left( {\frac{3}{2}} \right)}^2}}} = {\left( {\frac{2}{3}} \right)^2} = \frac{4}{9}$
Let m = mass of the body
If W and W_h be its weight at earth's surface and at a height h above the earth's surface, then
W = mg
${\text{and }}{{\text{W}}_h} = m{g_h} = m \times \frac{4}{9}g = \frac{4}{9}mg$
$ \Rightarrow {{\text{W}}_h} = m{g_h} = \frac{4}{9} \times 63 $ = 28N

Q7. A body weighed 250 N on the surface. Assuming the earth to be a sphere of uniform mass density, how much would it weigh halfway down to the centre of earth?
    (A) 195 N
    (B) 240 N
    (C) 210 N
    (D) 125 N   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: Weight of a body of mass m at the surface of earth's surface, W = mg = 250 N.
Body of mass m is located at depth,
d = $\frac{1}{2}$Re
where Re = Radius of earth
Acceleration due to gravity at depth g(d)is given by the relation:
g(d) = $\left(1-\frac{d}{R_{e}}\right) g$
$=\left(1-\frac{R_{e}}{2 \times R_{e}}\right) g=\frac{1}{2} g$
Weight of the body at depth d,
W' = mg(d)
$=\mathrm{m} \times \frac{1}{2} g=\frac{1}{2} m g=\frac{1}{2} W$
$=\frac{1}{2} \times 250$ = 125N

Q8. A rocket is fired vertically with a speed of 5 km sec^-1 ^ ^ from the earth’s surface. How far from the earth does the rocket go before returning to the earth? Mass of the earth = 6.0 $ \times $ 10^24 kg; mean radius of the earth = 6.4 $ \times $ 10^6 m; G = 6.67 $ \times $ 10^-11 N m^2 kg^-2.
    (A) 8.0 $ \times $ 10^6 m from the earth’s center   <-- CORRECT
    (B) 7.5 $ \times $ ${\rm{1}}{0^{\rm{6}}}$ m from the earth’s center
    (C) 8.5 $ \times $ 10^6 m from the earth’s centre
    (D) 9.0 $ \times $ 10^6 m from the earth’s centre
    Answer: A
    Explanation: Work done in bringing a mass m against a mass M at a distance 'x' through a distance dx 
= Gravitational force $\times$ Distance = $\frac{\mathrm{GMm}}{x^{2}} d x$
Work done in bringing it from $\infty$ to r = GMm$\left[\frac{1}{\infty}-\frac{1}{R}\right]$
$=-\mathrm{GMm}\left[\frac{1}{R}-\frac{1}{\infty}\right]$
Therefore Gravitational Potential Energy, $W =\frac{-\mathrm{GMm}}{R}$
Initial Total Energy = Kinetic Energy + Potential Energy
$=\frac{1}{2} \mathrm{mv}^{2}-\frac{\mathrm{GMm}}{R}$
Final Total Energy = 0 - $\frac{\mathrm{GMm}}{(R+h)}$
Applying law of conservation of energy:
$\frac{1}{2} \mathrm{mv}^{2}-\frac{\mathrm{GMm}}{R}=0-\frac{\mathrm{GMm}}{(R+h)}$
$\frac{\mathrm{GMm}}{(R+h)}=\frac{\mathrm{GMm}}{R}-\frac{1}{2} \mathrm{mv}^{2}$
$\frac{G M}{(R+h)}=\frac{1}{R}\left(G M-\frac{1}{2} R v^{2}\right)$
$\frac{(R+h)}{R}=\frac{G M}{\left(G M-\frac{1}{2} R v^{2}\right)}$
$(R+h)=\frac{G M R}{\left(G M-\frac{1}{2} R v^{2}\right)}$
h = $\frac{G M R}{\left(G M-\frac{1}{2} \mathrm{Rv}^{2}\right)}-R$
$h=\frac{\frac{1}{2} R^{2} v^{2}}{\left(G M-\frac{1}{2} R v^{2}\right)}$
$h=\frac{\left[\frac{1}{2} \times\left(6.4 \times 10^{6}\right)^{2} \times\left(5 \times 10^{3}\right)^{2}\right]}{\left[\left(6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times 6 \times 10^{24}\right)-\left(\frac{1}{2} \times 6.4 \times 10^{6} \times\left(5 \times 10^{3}\right)^{2}\right]\right.} $m
$=\frac{\left(512 \times 10^{18}\right)}{40 \times 10^{13}-80 \times 10^{12}}$
=1.6 × 10^6m
Height achieved by the rocket with respect to the centre of the Earth = Re + h
= 6.4 $\times$ 10^6 + 1.6 $\times$ 10^6 = 8 $\times$ 10^6 m.

Q9. The escape speed of a projectile on the earth’s surface is 11.2 km sec^-1. A body is projected out with thrice this speed. What is the speed of the body far away from the earth? Ignore the presence of the sun and other planets.

    (B) 28.7 km/s
    (C) 29.7 km/s
    (D) 32.7 km/s
    Answer: A
    Explanation: The escape velocity of a projectile from the Earth, V_esc = 11.2 km/s
Projection velocity of the projectile, V_p = 3V_esc
Mass of the projectile = m
The velocity of the projectile far away from the Earth = V_f
The total energy of the projectile on the earth$= \frac{1}{2}m{v_p}^2 - \frac{1}{2}m{v_{esc}}^2$ 
The gravitational potential energy of the projectile far away from the Earth is zero.
The total energy of the projectile far away from the Earth = $\frac{1}{2}m{v_f}^2$ 
From the law of conservation of energy, we have
$= \frac{1}{2}m{v_p}^2 - \frac{1}{2}m{v_{esc}}^2 = \frac{1}{2}m{v_f}^2$ 
${v_f} = \sqrt {{v_p}^2 - {v_{esc}}^2}$ 
$ = \sqrt {{{\left( {3{v_{esc}}} \right)}^2} - {{\left( {{v_{esc}}} \right)}^2}}$ 
$= \sqrt {{{\left( {3{v_{esc}}} \right)}^2} - {{\left( {{v_{esc}}} \right)}^2}}$
$=\sqrt 8 {v_{esc}}$
$=\sqrt 8 \times 11.2 $ = 31.68 km/sec

    (B) 6.0 $ \times $ 10^9 J
    (C) 6.2 $ \times $ 10^9 J

    Explanation: Mass of the Earth, M = 6.0 $\times$ 10^24kg
Mass of the satellite, m = 200 kg
Radius of the Earth, Re = 6.4 $\times$ 10^6m Universal gravitational constant, G = 6.67 $\times$ 10^-11N m^2 kg^−2
Height of the satellite, h = 400 km = 4 $\times$ 10^5m = 0.4 $\times$ 106m
Total energy of the satellite at height = $1\over2$mv^2 + $\left(\frac{-G M_{e} m}{R_{e}+h}\right)$
Orbital velocity of the satellite, $v=\sqrt{\frac{G M_{e}}{R_{e}+h}}$
Total energy at height, $h=\frac{1}{2} m\left(\frac{G M_{e}}{R_{e}+h}\right)-\frac{G M_{e} m}{R_{e}+h}=-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{-G M_{e} m}{R_{e}+h}\right)$

Q13. Two satellites are in circular orbits around a planet that has radius 9.00 $ \times $ ${\rm{1}}{0^{\rm{6}}}$ m. One satellite has mass 68.0 kg, orbital radius 5.00 $ \times $ ${\rm{1}}{0^{\rm{7}}}$ m, and orbital speed 4800 m/s. The second satellite has a mass 84.0 kg and an orbital radius of 3.00 $ \times $ ${\rm{1}}{0^{\rm{7}}}$ m. What is the orbital speed of this second satellite?
    (A) 6122 m/s
    (B) 6152 m/s
    (C) 6132 m/s
    (D) 6192 m/s   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: We know $T=\frac{2 \pi R}{v}$
$\Rightarrow \frac{\left(\frac{2 \pi R_{1}}{v_{1}}\right)^{2}}{R_{1}^{3}}=\frac{\left(\frac{2 \pi R_{2}}{v_{2}}\right)^{2}}{R_{2}^{3}}$
$\Rightarrow \frac{4 \pi^{2}}{v_{1}^{2} R_{1}}=\frac{4 \pi^{2}}{v_{2}^{2} R_{2}}$
$\Rightarrow v_{1}^{2} R_{1}=v_{2}^{2} R_{2}$
$\Rightarrow \frac{v_{1}^{2} R_1}{R_{2}}=v_{2}^{2}$
$\Rightarrow \sqrt{\frac{v_{1}^{2} R_1}{R_{2}}}=v_{2}$
Here v_1 = 4800 m/sec; R_1 = 5 $\times$ 10^7m, R_2 = 3 $\times$ 10^7m, v^2 = ?
$\Rightarrow \sqrt{\frac{(4800)^{2} \times 5 \times 10^{7}}{3 \times 10^{7}}}=v_{2}$
$\Rightarrow$ 4800 $\times$ 1.29 = 6192 m/sec.

Q15. Deimos, a moon of Mars, is about 12 km in diameter with mass 2.0 $ \times $ 10^15 kg. Suppose you are stranded alone on Deimos and want to play a one-person game of baseball. Q1. Two stars each of one solar mass (= 2 $ \times $ 10^30 kg) are approaching each other for a head-on collision. When they are a distance 10^9 km, their speeds are negligible. What is the speed with which they collide? The radius of each star is 10^4 km. assume the stars to remain undistorted until they collide. (Use the known value of G).
    (A) 2.8 $ \times $ 10^6 m/s
    (B) 2.2 $ \times $ 10^6 m/s
    (C) 2.6 $ \times $ 10^6 m/s   <-- CORRECT
    (D) 1.6 $ \times $ 10^6 m/s
    Answer: C
    Explanation: Let $\vec v_1$ and $\vec v_2$ be the velocities of two stars when they collide. 
According to the law of conservation of momentum,
$M{{\vec v}_1} + M{{\vec v}_2} = 0$
$\Rightarrow {{\vec v}_1} = - {{\vec v}_2}$
$\Rightarrow {v_1} = {v_2} = v$
According to law of conservation of energy,
${\text{2}}\left[ {\frac{1}{2}M{v^2}} \right] = G{M^2}\left[ {\frac{1}{{{r_2}}} - \frac{1}{{{r_1}}}} \right]$
We Have
Mass of the star,M = 2 $\times$ 10^30 kg
Distance between the stars, r_1 = 10^9 km = 10^12 m
Radius of star, r = 10^4km = 10^7m
The distance between two stars when they collide is,
r_2 = 2r = 2 $\times$ 10^7m
$\therefore$ The speed with which the stars collide is given by,
$v = \sqrt {GM\left[ {\frac{1}{{{r_2}}} - \frac{1}{{{r_1}}}} \right]}$
$v = \sqrt {6.67 \times {{10}^{ - 11}} \times 2 \times {{10}^{30}} \times \left[ {\frac{1}{{2 \times {{10}^7}}} - \frac{1}{{{{10}^{12}}}}} \right]}$
v = 2.6 $\times$ 10^6m/sec

Q2. Two heavy spheres each of mass 100 kg and radius 0.10 m are placed 1.0 m apart on a horizontal table. What is the gravitational force and potential at the midpoint of the line joining the centers of the spheres? Is an object placed at that point in equilibrium? If so, is the equilibrium stable or unstable?
    (A) 0, 2.7 $ \times $ ${\rm{1}}{0^{ - {\rm{8}}}}$ J/kg, stable
    (B) 0, 1.9 $ \times $ 10^−8 J/kg, unstable
    (C) 0, 1.9 $ \times $ 10^−8 J/kg stable
    (D) 0, 2.7 $ \times $ 10^-8 J/kg, unstable   <-- CORRECT
    Answer: D
    Explanation: Here, G = 6.67 $\times$ 10^−11N m^2 kg^−2
M = 100 kg
R = 0.1 m
Distance between the two spheres d = 1.0 m
Suppose that the distance of either sphere from the midpoint of the line joining their centre is r. Then
r = $d\over2$ = 0.5 m
The gravitational field at the midpoint due to two spheres will be equal and opposite.
Hence, the resultant gravitational field at the midpoint = 0
The gravitational potential at the midpoint.
$=\left(\frac{-G M}{r}\right) \times 2$
$=-\frac{6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times 100 \times 2}{0.5}$ 
= −2.7 $\times$ 10^−8 Jkg^-1
As the effective force on the body placed at the midpoint is zero, so the body is in equilibrium, if the body is displaced a little towards either mass body from its equilibrium position, it will not return to its initial position of equilibrium. Hence, the body is in unstable equilibrium.

Q15. Derive an expression for the work required to move an Earth satellite of mass m from a circular orbit of radius 2 ${{\rm{R}}_{\rm{E}}}$ to one of radius 3 ${{\rm{R}}_{\rm{E}}}$.
    (A) $\frac{G M_E}{12 R_E}$
    (B) $\frac{G M_E m}{24 R_E}$
    (C) $\frac{G m}{24 R_E}$
    (D) $\frac{G M_E m}{12 R E}$   <-- CORRECT

    Explanation: The energy of a satellite in low orbit:
$E_{L}=\frac{-G M m}{2 r}=\frac{-G M_{E} m}{2\left(2 R_{E}\right)}=\frac{-G M_{E} m}{4 R_{E}} $
The energy of a satellite in high orbit:
$$$E_{H}=\frac{-G M m}{2 r}=\frac{-G M_{E} m}{2\left(3 R_{E}\right)}=\frac{-G M_{E} m}{6 R_{E}}$
Energy required to move a satellite from the orbit of radius 2R_E to orbit of radius 3R_E:
$=\Delta E$ = E_H − E_L = $\frac{-G M_{E} m}{6 R_{E}}-\left(\frac{-G M_{E} m}{4 R_{E}}\right) \Rightarrow \Delta E=\frac{G M_{E} m}{12 R_{E}}$
