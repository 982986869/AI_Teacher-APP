Consider the reaction,

 $2{C_4}{H_{10}}(g) + 13{O_2}(g) \to 8C{O_2}(g) + 10{H_2}O(l)$ 

If 300g of C_(4)H_(10) (g) is burnt with 1000 g of O_(2), the yield of H_(2)O and the limiting reagent in the reaction are _____ and _____ respectively.
   (A) 350 g and C_(4) H_(10)
   (B) 465 g and O_(2)  <-- correct
   (C) 400 g and O_(2)
   (D) 435 g and O_(2)

Answer: 465 g and O_(2)
Explanation:
Calculations

As per the stoichiometry of the given equation,

 $2{C_4}{H_{10}}(g) + 13{O_2}(g) \to 8C{O_2}(g) + 10{H_2}O(l)$ 

Calculations:

Step 1/ -

Molar mass of C_(4)H_(10)

= (4 $\times$ 2 + 1 $\times$ 10) amu = (48 + 10) amu

= 58 amu

 $\therefore$ 300g of C_(4)H_(10)

= $\frac{300}{58}$ moles

= 5.17 moles

Step 2 / -

 For the yield of H_(2)O;

 Since 2 mols of C_(4)H_(10) yields 10 H_(2)O i.e.

 = 10 moles.

 $\therefore$ 5.17 moles of C_(4)H_(10) would yield 10H_(2)O_( )

 $ = \frac{{5.17 \times 10}}{2}moles$ 

 = 25.85 moles

 $ = (25.85 \times 18)g$ 

 465.3 g $\approx$ 465 g
 For predicting the limiting reagent;
 
 Calculate the number of moles of O_(2) needed to react with 5.17 moles of C_(4)H_(10)

 Since 2 moles of C_(4)H_(10) requires 13 O_(2) molecules i.e.

 = 13 moles

 $ = \frac{{13 \times 5.17}}{2}moles$ 

 = 33.62 moles

 Number of moles of O_(2 )actually taken for combustion

 = $\frac{mass \ of \ oxygen \ taken }{ molar \ mass \ of \ oxygen}$ 

 = $\frac{1000}{32} moles$ 

 = 31.25 moles

 Thus, it is noteworthy that,

 O_(2(g)) taken for reaction (i.e. 31.25 moles) is lesser than the required number of moles (i.e. 33.62 moles).

 Hence, it would be consumed first and is, therefore, the limiting reagent for this reaction.
