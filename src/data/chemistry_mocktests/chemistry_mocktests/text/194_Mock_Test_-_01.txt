Q2. (id 62019) [MCQ]
Find the energy of the photon which has a wavelength of 0.50 $\mathop A\limits^ \circ $ ?

(Hint: h = Planck’s constant = 6.626 $\times$ 10^(-34) J)
   (A) 0.98 $\times$ 10^(-15) J
   (B) 3.98 $\times$ 10^(-15) J  <-- correct
   (C) 2.98 $\times$ 10^(-15) J
   (D) 1.98 $\times$ 10^(-15) J

Answer: 3.98 $\times$ 10^(-15) J
Explanation:
Energy (E) of a photon having wavelength ( $\lambda$ ) is given by the expression, $E={hc\over \lambda}$ 

where, h = Planck’s constant = 6.626 $\times$ 10^(-34)

c = velocity of light in vacuum = 3 $\times$ 10^(8) m/s 

Wavelength $\lambda = 0.50 A^° = 0.50\times 10^{-10} m$ $\lambda$ = 0.50 Å = 0.50 $\times$ 10^(-10) m

Substituting the values in the given expression of E:

 $6.626\times 10^{-34}\times 3\times 10^8\over 0.50\times 10^{-10}$ = 3.98 $\times$ 10^(-15) J

Q37. (id 68843) [MCQ]
Considering the elements B, C, N, F and Si, the correct order of their non-metallic character is:
   (A) F > N > C > B >Si  <-- correct
   (B) B > C > Si > N > F
   (C) Si > C > B > N > F
   (D) F >N > C > Si > B

Answer: F > N > C > B >Si
Explanation:
In a period, the non-metallic character increases from left to right. Thus, among B, C, N and F, non-metallic character decreases in the order: F > N > C > B. However, within a group, non-metallic character decreases from top to bottom. Thus, C is more non-metallic than Si. Therefore, the correct sequence of decreasing non-metallic character is: F > N > C > B > Si.

The same can be verified with first ionization potential of the atoms. The values are F (17.42) > N (14.53) > C (11.26) > B (8.298) > Si (8.151). Lower ionization potential implies easy tendency to loose electrons. The potential of B and Si appears to be close but it is the bigger size of Si that helps it loose electron comparatively faster.

   (A) $\bar \nu = 109,737{\text{}}\left( {\frac{1}{{{\text{n}}_1^2}} - {\text{}}\frac{1}{{{\text{n}}_2^2}}} \right)$  <-- correct
   (B) $\bar \nu = 109,677{\text{}}\left( {\frac{1}{{{\text{n}}_1^4}} - {\text{}}\frac{1}{{{\text{n}}_2^2}}} \right)$

   (D) $\bar \nu = 109,777{\text{}}\left( {\frac{1}{{{\text{n}}_1^2}} - {\text{}}\frac{1}{{{\text{n}}_2^3}}} \right)$

Answer: $\bar \nu = 109,737{\text{}}\left( {\frac{1}{{{\text{n}}_1^2}} - {\text{}}\frac{1}{{{\text{n}}_2^2}}} \right)$
Explanation:
In an amazing demonstration of mathematical insight, in 1885 Balmer came up with a simple formula for predicting the wavelength of any of the lines in atomic Hydrogen in what we now know as the Balmer series.

Three years later, Rydberg generalized this so that it was possible to determine the wavelengths of any of the lines in the hydrogen emission spectrum. Rydberg suggested that all atomic spectra formed families with this pattern (he was unaware of Balmer's work).

It turns out that there are families of spectra following Rydberg's pattern, notably in the alkali metals, sodium, potassium, etc., but not with the precision the hydrogen atom lines fit the Balmer formula, and low values of n_(2) predicted wavelengths that deviate considerably.

Rydberg's general equation is as follows:

 $\overline \nu = {1\over \lambda}=R_h\left ({1\over n_1^2}-{1\over n_2^2}\right)$ 

where

 R_(H) is the Rydberg constant and is 1,09,677 cm^(-1)
 n_(1) and n_(2) are integers (whole numbers) with n_(2)>n_(1)
